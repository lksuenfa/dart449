The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations actually distinguishes “food loss” occurring at the production, postharvest and processing stages in the food supply chain from “food waste” which occurs at the retailer and consumer level.

This distinction between the unavoidable connotation of the term “food loss” versus the actionable one of “food waste” has been built into a biased global narrative according to which ...

In developping countries, food is primarily lost at earlier stages of the supply chain due to poor technical infrastructure

While rich countries produce food waste due to wasteful consumer habits;
successfully shifting the focus from systemic and institutional problems to the consumer side, the individual citizen.

Even international efforts to tackle the food waste crisis underestimate the impact the contribution of production and supply chains could have.

The United Nations’ 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development set the target to “halve per capita global food waste at the retail and consumer levels and reduce food losses along production and supply chains, including post-harvest losses”, target to which Canada has committed itself to in 2015 .

While the target for the consumer and retail side is specific and measurable, there is a lesser emphasis on production and supply chains whose target can only be described as vague at most.

From producers...

There is currently a gap in food loss studies in the global North and this issue is actually less well understood there than in developing countries. Farmers’ opinions are specially underrepresented in food loss studies resulting in policies and solutions which might undermine their interests. It is likely that food loss at the production stage might be connected to unfair trading practices from buyers encouraging over-production, selection according to aesthetic qualities, financial risks, and various imbalances in market power and volatility. Buyers have a huge power as the only 5 big multinational corporations command 80% of the Canadian market.

Unfair trading practices like short notice cancellations and unilateral contract changes by buyers have been identified as a major cause of food loss.

However, solutions to reduce food loss on the farm in Canada tend to focus on charitable actions such as encouraging food recovery or through tax incentives for donations. In reality, many farmers feel that the 25% tax credit offered did not offset the logistical costs of donating surplus food and the charitable sector faces significant logistical challenges due to limited infrastucture which makes it difficult to manage the high volumes of produce.

The overly complicated paperwork makes it very difficult for polyculture farmers with diverse crops to claim tax creditsas as the amount of each type of food needs to be specified. It is thus a process favoring monocultures, which are more wasteful and less sustainable.

To manufacturers...

The implementation of overly conservative data labels by manufacturers to protect their brand and manage consumer perceptions of the product’s quality has gradually turned into a significant driver of food waste. A best before date is not an expiry date and has little to do with food safety. If products are close to or past the date label assigned to them, some retail and foodservice customers have the option to return the product to the manufacturer at no cost to them. Even though a manufactured product’s date code can be highly conservative and the food perfectly safe to eat, some manufacturers will not donate this food.
